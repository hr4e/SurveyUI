Although breast cancer is common, in most cases we cannot identify a hereditary cause. However, women whose close relatives have breast or ovarian cancer have a higher chance of developing these cancers. In addition, women who develop breast or ovarian cancer at a younger age are at higher risk of developing a second cancer. The good news is that women at higher risk of breast and ovarian cancer can take steps to reduce their chances of developing a first or recurrent cancer.

What can be done to find out if I’m at higher risk of a first or second cancer?

Blood tests can detect changes (mutations) in certain genes that explain some of this higher risk. The genes are called BRCA1 and BRCA2. Each woman should consider her particular situation, including her own history of cancer or precancerous lesions and her family history of cancer. She should discuss this with her physician or cancer risk genetic counselor before deciding whether to have BRCA testing.

If BRCA mutations are found ...

... we can estimate the chances of a first or second breast or ovarian cancer.

If no BRCA mutations are found ...

... the interpretation of this result depends on what is known about the BRCA mutation status of a woman’s family members with breast or ovarian cancer. If family members with cancer were known to have BRCA mutations, then the woman who does not have such mutations has a typical, not increased, risk of such cancer. If family members with cancer were tested and did not have BRCA mutations, then the woman under consideration and her family may have increased risk of cancer, from mutations that medical technology is currently unable to detect. Finally, if BRCA testing has not been done on family members with breast or ovarian cancer, then finding no mutations in a woman is called an “uninformative negative.” This means that we cannot fully interpret this test result, since we don’t know whether BRCA mutations that are present but not tested account for the cancers in her family.

If I am found to be at higher risk, what can I do?
To lower the chance of first or recurrent breast cancer, women may take medication to block estrogen's effect in the breast or have her breasts removed (mastectomy). For women who do not choose mastectomy, yearly mammograms or breast MRI will be recommended. To lower the chance of ovarian cancer, high risk woman may take birth control pills for at least five years or have their ovaries removed.

Breast Cancer:

Breast cancer is very common, affecting 1 in 8 American women during their lifetime. While most breast cancers occur in women without a family history of breast cancer, about 10% of breast cancers occur in women with BRCA gene mutations. Without preventive steps, up to 85% of women with BRCA mutations will develop breast cancer by age 80. Men with mutations in the BRCA2 gene have a 5% to 10% chance of developing breast cancer.

Current Screening for Breast Cancer:

Current screening options include breast exam, mammogram, and breast MRI. For most women, a mammogram is the best test for early breast cancer detection. Breast MRI is recommended for women who have any of the following risk factors:

  1. BRCA mutation,
  2. strong family history of breast or ovarian cancer-- even if BRCA mutations have not been detected,
  3. chest radiation between ages 10 and 30, or
  4. a rare genetic syndrome that increases breast cancer risk.

Current Screening for Ovarian Cancer:

There are currently no reliable tests to screen women for ovarian cancer. Pelvic examinations (feeling the ovaries through the vagina), trans-vaginal ultrasound (viewing the ovaries through a probe inserted in the vagina), and blood tests (CA 125) have not been proven to find ovarian cancer early enough to reduce death rates.

How does BRCA mutation status affect recommendations for women who do not have breast cancer?
  1. If a family member with breast or ovarian cancer is BRCA positive while the unaffected woman is BRCA negative: then the unaffected woman did not inherit the abnormal gene, and she carries the same risk of developing breast cancer as other women at "average risk" -- about 1 in 8 chance of developing breast cancer during her lifetime. Such women should have routine screening for breast cancer.
  2. If both the affected relatives and the unaffected woman are BRCA positive: then the unaffected woman may have up to an 85% chance of developing breast or ovarian cancer. Breast screening through breast MRI and mammograms and preventive measures (medication, mastectomy, or ovary removal) should be considered.
  3. If neither the affected relatives nor the unaffected woman are BRCA positive: then risk estimations are less accurate, because the family may carry gene mutations that current technology cannot detect. The chance of developing breast or ovarian cancer may be 55% (depending on the family history). Breast MRI and mammogram should be considered. Preventive measures (medication, mastectomy, or ovary removal may be recommended.

Health insurance concerns about BRCA test results

The Federal Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act of 2008 (GINA) prohibits health insurance carriers from using a person’s genetic information to determine insurance eligibility or premiums. GINA also prevents employers from making decisions about hiring, firing, and job assignment based on genetic information and prevents employers from requesting genetic information about employees.

Life insurance and BRCA test results

Information about pre-existing medical conditions or risks is used routinely by life insurance underwriters to determine eligibility for coverage and to set premiums. An individual's failure to disclose such information is illegal. The extent to which such genetic information is being gathered by life insurance companies or used to determine eligibility or premiums is unknown.

WE CARE Collaborative: WEstern CAncer Risk Evaluation

A collaboration between Palo Alto Medical Foundation and Intermountain Healthcare dedicated to identifying people with increased chances of developing cancer and and informing them of measures they can take to reduce that risk.

Ovarian cancer

Cancer of the ovary is cancer arising from the ovary, the organ that produces eggs in females. The ovary has several types of cells. Cancer in the “epithelial” cells of the ovary, is associated with BRCA mutations. Up to 60% of women (6 in 10) who get epithelial ovarian cancer have a mutation in the BRCA genes.

Cancer of the fallopian tubes

Cancers in some organs near the ovary also more commonly have BRCA mutations, including the fallopian tubes (which carries eggs from the ovary to the uterus).

Cancer of the peritoneum (the lining of the abdominal cavity)

Cancers in some organs near the ovary also more commonly have BRCA mutations, including the peritoneum (a lining that covers the abdominal organs).

We group these three types of cancer (epithelial ovarian, primary peritoneal, and fallopian tube cancers) together when discussing “ovarian cancer” that is associated with BRCA mutations.
